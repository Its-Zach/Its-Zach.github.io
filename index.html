Treasure Valley Water Hub

Overview

The Treasure Valley Water Hub (TVWH) is a platform designed to help users find relevant and trustworthy resources about water management in the Treasure Valley. The site features responsive water maps, 3D interactive visualizations, and serves as a gateway to federal and local information sources.

My Role

I was responsible for designing the platform's UI/UX, conducting user testing, and modeling the interactive assets. Over a four-month collaboration with the TVWH team and stakeholders, we developed the final live build, which received a positive response and is set to grow alongside the organization.

Artifact Overview

Below, you’ll find the various development stages of the TVWH landing page. The progression showcases how the wireframe evolved into a more complex and refined design, highlighting key updates and iterations based on user and client feedback. Further below, I’ve included designer commentary and workflow notes to provide deeper insight into the development process.

Page Styling

With layout changes being made often so did page and content styling. As we moved into further iterations a shift away from abstract imagery to a more image based platform seemed necessary. The client wanted the experience to feel centered around the local Treasure Valley area and not feel generalized. Carefully placed images integrated perfectly into the workflow of the site and provided a grounded experience. Another hurdle in styling was balancing the very text based content with visuals to keep the user engaged without feeling overwhelmed or confused. Along with the strategies discussed in the colors section above, pages remained simple with clear separations of content, usability features, and a uniform design standard across the site.

Content Layout

The site’s content also changed dramatically resulting in the “Cafeteria Menu” landing page having to adapt. The above iterations can see the introduction to iconography, various verbiage changes, and many different layouts for the top categories. User testing was performed often on these iterations and resulted in what we see in our final product today.

Colors

From the beginning of the project, our clients didn't have a set color palette, which resulted in a lot of trial and error and is one of the more visible changes that happened throughout the design. First, wanting to avoid blue “traditional water” tones and seek out colors not seen in their peers, the client introduced a few palettes, including greens, pinks, and purples. After seeing these pallets within the site context, they agreed with me in scaling the color use back and using the saturated colors more scarcely. This is seen in the final design, which maintains neutral cream backgrounds using bolder colors to highlight specific areas of interest.
